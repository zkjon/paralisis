---
title: definition
description: a precise explanation of what sleep paralysis is from a neurological and functional perspective
---

sleep paralysis is a temporary state of dissociation between consciousness and voluntary motor control that occurs during the transition between sleep and wakefulness.

in this state, the person is conscious but unable to move or speak voluntarily.

---

## a functional desynchronization

to understand it precisely, it must be seen as a problem of synchronization between systems.

the brain does not switch the body from “asleep” to “awake” instantly, but through coordinated processes that regulate:

- consciousness  
- muscular activity  
- sensory perception  

under normal conditions, these systems change state together.

in sleep paralysis, this coordination temporarily breaks down: some systems “wake up” while others remain in sleep mode.

---

## consciousness without motor control

one of the central elements of this phenomenon is the separation between two key functions:

- consciousness → becomes active and allows awareness of the environment  
- voluntary motor control → remains inhibited  

this creates a situation in which the person:

- knows they are awake  
- attempts to move, speak, or react  
- cannot execute any voluntary action  

it is important to understand that this inability is not passive.

the body is not “failing”; it is being actively inhibited by mechanisms associated with sleep.

---

## muscle atonia

during sleep, especially in certain phases, the body enters a state known as **muscle atonia**.

this state involves:

- near-complete inhibition of voluntary muscles  
- preservation of automatic functions (breathing, heartbeat)  

its function is protective: it prevents the body from physically acting out movements that occur during dreams.

sleep paralysis occurs when this atonia does not deactivate at the same time as consciousness.

---

## a transitional state

sleep paralysis is not a stable condition, but a transitional one.

it typically occurs at specific moments:

- when falling asleep (the body falls asleep before the mind)  
- when waking up (the mind becomes active before the body)  

this places it in an intermediate state, where the boundaries between sleeping and being awake are not fully defined.

---

## not a dream nor a loss of consciousness

to define this phenomenon correctly, it is important to distinguish it from other states:

- it is not a dream → awareness of the environment is present  
- it is not a nightmare → it does not occur within the dream, but during the transition  
- it is not a loss of consciousness → the person remains aware  
- it is not a permanent neurological paralysis → it is temporary and reversible  

this distinction helps clarify that it is not a motor disorder, but a specific state within the sleep cycle.

---

## a hybrid state

sleep paralysis can be understood as a hybrid state in which elements of two systems coexist:

- features of sleep → immobility, motor inhibition  
- features of wakefulness → consciousness, environmental awareness  

this overlap creates an experience that does not fully belong to either category.

---

## limits of the phenomenon

although the definition is clear in general terms, there are variations in how it presents:

- the level of consciousness may vary  
- the clarity of environmental perception may differ  
- the duration may be brief or extend for several seconds or minutes  

these differences do not change the nature of the phenomenon, but they do affect how it is experienced.

---

## an operational definition

in functional terms, sleep paralysis can be defined as:

> a transient state in which consciousness is active while voluntary motor control remains inhibited due to the persistence of sleep-related muscle atonia, resulting in a dissociation between perception and action.
